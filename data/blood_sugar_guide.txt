BLOOD SUGAR MANAGEMENT GUIDE

TARGET BLOOD SUGAR RANGES
For most adults with diabetes, target blood sugar levels are:
- Before meals (fasting): 80 to 130 mg/dL
- Two hours after meals: less than 180 mg/dL
- At bedtime: 100 to 140 mg/dL
- HbA1c (3-month average): less than 7 percent
Normal fasting blood sugar for people without diabetes is 70 to 100 mg/dL. Pre-diabetes is 100 to 125 mg/dL. Diabetes is diagnosed at 126 mg/dL or higher on two separate tests.

HOW OFTEN TO TEST BLOOD SUGAR
Type 1 diabetes: Test 4 to 10 times per day - before each meal, before bed, before and after exercise, and when you feel unwell.
Type 2 diabetes on insulin: Test 2 to 4 times daily as directed by your doctor.
Type 2 diabetes not on insulin: Test once daily or as recommended, often in the morning before eating.
All diabetics: Test more frequently when sick, changing medications, or during pregnancy.
Use a continuous glucose monitor (CGM) if recommended by your doctor for real-time tracking.

WHAT TO DO IF BLOOD SUGAR IS TOO HIGH (HYPERGLYCEMIA)
High blood sugar is above 180 mg/dL after meals or above 130 mg/dL before meals.
Immediate steps:
1. Drink plenty of water to help flush excess glucose through urine.
2. Take your prescribed diabetes medication or insulin as directed.
3. Avoid eating sugary foods, white bread, rice, pasta, and sugary drinks.
4. Do light exercise such as a 15-minute walk to help lower blood sugar - but NOT if ketones are present.
5. Check for ketones using a urine strip if blood sugar is above 240 mg/dL.
6. If ketones are present, do not exercise - contact your doctor immediately.
7. Monitor blood sugar every 1 to 2 hours until it returns to target range.
Call emergency services or go to the emergency room if: blood sugar is above 400 mg/dL, you have vomiting, difficulty breathing, fruity breath odor, or confusion - these are signs of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), a medical emergency.

WHAT TO DO IF BLOOD SUGAR IS TOO LOW (HYPOGLYCEMIA)
Low blood sugar is below 70 mg/dL. Symptoms include shakiness, sweating, dizziness, confusion, and rapid heartbeat.
Immediate steps - follow the 15-15 rule:
1. Eat or drink 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates immediately:
   - 4 glucose tablets
   - 4 ounces (half cup) of fruit juice or regular soda (not diet)
   - 1 tablespoon of honey or sugar
   - Hard candies - check label for 15 grams of carbs
2. Wait 15 minutes, then recheck blood sugar.
3. If still below 70 mg/dL, repeat step 1.
4. Once blood sugar is above 70 mg/dL, eat a small snack with protein if your next meal is more than 1 hour away (e.g., peanut butter crackers, cheese).
5. Do not drive or operate machinery until blood sugar is above 90 mg/dL.
If the person is unconscious or cannot swallow: do not give food or drink. Call emergency services (911) immediately. If glucagon is available, administer it as instructed.

SYMPTOMS OF HIGH BLOOD SUGAR (HYPERGLYCEMIA)
- Frequent urination
- Increased thirst
- Blurred vision
- Headache
- Fatigue and weakness
- Difficulty concentrating
- Slow-healing wounds
- Fruity-smelling breath (sign of ketoacidosis - seek emergency care)

SYMPTOMS OF LOW BLOOD SUGAR (HYPOGLYCEMIA)
- Shakiness or trembling
- Sweating
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Irritability, anxiety, or mood changes
- Hunger
- Pale skin
- Blurred vision
- Confusion or difficulty concentrating
- Seizures or loss of consciousness (severe - call 911)

PREVENTING HIGH BLOOD SUGAR
- Follow a consistent meal schedule and eat balanced meals with controlled carbohydrates.
- Take medications and insulin at the prescribed times.
- Exercise regularly - aim for 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.
- Monitor blood sugar as directed.
- Limit alcohol, which can cause unpredictable blood sugar changes.
- Manage stress, as stress hormones raise blood sugar.

PREVENTING LOW BLOOD SUGAR
- Never skip meals or delay eating for more than 4 to 5 hours.
- Always carry fast-acting sugar (glucose tablets, juice) with you.
- If drinking alcohol, eat food at the same time.
- Tell family and friends about hypoglycemia symptoms so they can help.
- Wear a medical alert bracelet identifying you as diabetic.
- Check blood sugar before driving.
- Discuss medication adjustments with your doctor if low blood sugar is frequent.

DIABETES DIET AND FOOD CHOICES
Best foods for blood sugar control:
- Non-starchy vegetables: broccoli, spinach, peppers, tomatoes, cucumber
- Whole grains: brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat bread (in controlled portions)
- Lean proteins: chicken, fish, tofu, eggs, legumes
- Healthy fats: avocado, nuts, olive oil
- Low-glycemic fruits: berries, apples, pears, oranges (in moderation)
Foods to limit or avoid:
- Sugary drinks: soda, juice, sports drinks, energy drinks
- Sweets: candy, cakes, cookies, ice cream
- White refined carbohydrates: white bread, white rice, pasta
- Fried and fatty foods
- Alcohol (especially on an empty stomach)
Eat soluble fiber foods like oat bran, beans, lentils, apples, and citrus fruits - they slow carbohydrate digestion and improve glucose control.

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR URGENTLY
Seek immediate medical care if you experience:
- Blood sugar above 400 mg/dL that does not come down with medication
- Signs of diabetic ketoacidosis: fruity breath, vomiting, heavy breathing, confusion
- Loss of consciousness due to low blood sugar
- Seizures
- Chest pain or difficulty breathing
- Signs of diabetic shock: unresponsiveness, seizure, inability to swallow

EXERCISE AND BLOOD SUGAR
Exercise generally lowers blood sugar by helping muscles use glucose.
Before exercise: Check blood sugar. If below 90 mg/dL, eat a carbohydrate snack first.
During long exercise: Recheck every 30 minutes and carry fast-acting sugar.
After exercise: Blood sugar may drop for up to 24 hours - check regularly.
Avoid intense exercise when blood sugar is above 240 mg/dL and ketones are present.

MEDICATIONS FOR DIABETES
Common diabetes medications include:
- Metformin: first-line medication for type 2 diabetes, lowers glucose production in liver
- Insulin: essential for type 1 diabetes, used in type 2 when other medications are not enough
- Sulfonylureas (e.g., glipizide, glyburide): stimulate insulin production
- SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., empagliflozin): help kidneys remove excess glucose through urine
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g., semaglutide, liraglutide): slow digestion and lower blood sugar
Always take medications as prescribed. Never adjust insulin doses without consulting your doctor.
